Bryce Canyon National Park

A Land of Forest and Hoodoos

Bryce Canyon is best known for its hoodoos, the colorful and oddly-shaped pillars of rocks, and represents the largest collection of them in the world.

Ebenezer Bryce, a homesteader from 1875-1880 and the area's namesake, was said to show visitors the Canyon and remark:

It's a helluva place to lose a cow.

The geologic term hoodoo, is from early geologists who thought the rock formations could cast spells with their magical spires and towering arches.

Technically, Bryce in not a true canyon (as those are carved by flowing water), but a series of amphitheaters. That said, water is still the erosive force behind the geological formations in the form of "frost-wedging" and chemical weathering.

For 200 days a year the temperature goes above and below freezing every day. During the day, melt water seeps into fractures only to freeze at night, expanding by 9%. As ice, it exerts a tremendous force (2,000-20,000 pounds per square inch). Over time this "frost-wedging" shatters and pries rock apart.

The erosional force of frost-wedging and the dissolving power of rainwater has shaped the colorful limestone rock of the Claron Formation into bizarre shapes including slot canyons, windows, fins, and spires.

Bryce Canyon National Park has eight marked and maintained trails for day hiking ranging from easy to strenuous, in addition to several overnight options requiring a backcountry permit. In total there are 50 miles of trails in the park. Other activities, such as snowshoeing and horseback riding, are available certain times of year.

As a Class I air quality area, Bryce Canyon offers great visability on clear days, extending over 100 miles. The park also has a 7.4 magnitude night sky, making it one of the darkest in North America. Stargazers can see 7,500 stars with the naked eye, versus the usual 2,000 in most places due to light pollution.

As a highland plateau, Bryce gets much more rain than the lowlands below and stays cooler during hot summers. The result is an ecosystems that supports a wide array of plants and animals, most notably the Douglas fir and Ponderosa pine forests.
